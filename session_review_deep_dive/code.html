Quadratic Equations Deep Dive

event Oct 24, 2023 functions Advanced Mathematics

Can you help me understand how the discriminant affects the roots of a quadratic equation? I'm specifically looking at the equation 2x² - 4x + 2 = 0.

10:15 AM check_circle
smart_toy
Digital Mentor

Absolutely, Alex. The discriminant is the part of the quadratic formula under the square root sign: b² - 4ac. It determines the nature of the roots without having to solve the whole equation.

Calculation for your equation:

Given: a = 2, b = -4, c = 2

D = (-4)² - 4(2)(2)

D = 16 - 16 = 0

Because D = 0, this equation has exactly one real root (a repeated root).

In a graphical sense, this means the vertex of your parabola just touches the x-axis at exactly one point.

10:16 AM

That makes sense! What would happen if the result was negative, like -4?

10:18 AM check_circle
smart_toy
Digital Mentor

Great question! If the discriminant is negative (D < 0), it means there are no real roots. Instead, you get two complex (imaginary) roots.

Visually, the parabola would be "floating" above or below the x-axis, never actually crossing it.
